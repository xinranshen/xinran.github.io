Week 2:

It’s interesting to see that the two readings, although 65 years apart, seem to echo with each other in a lot of ways. Bush brought up the idea of a memory extension machine ( which, in my opinion, kind of resembles a google drive) that enables individuals to store, manage and share information and data as well as accessing other people’s knowledge base through an interconnected system, which, in his opinion, would assist humans intellectually and become a new tendency of technological advancement after WWII. Bush’s predictions were realized in some ways in the decentralized era of Web as described in Tim Berners-Lee’s article, but Lee points out that although the internet provides a platform for users to access and share information more efficiently, the access is not equal depending on the individual user’s socioeconomic status, ISP, geographical locations, and more.

Although both authors desire a world where information can be shared efficiently and accelerate innovation, I was reminded of big tech companies like Google which potentially harm the privacy of individual users by exploiting user data logs for ad revenues, as described by surveillance capitalism, a phrase brought up by Shoshana Zuboff. As residents of the Internet, while information creates value we never think of, it also puts us at risk of being totally transparent and losing sovereignty over the information we wish to store safely.
